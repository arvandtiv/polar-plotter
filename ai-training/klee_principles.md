# Klee's creative ways — the method companion to the rules

Where a **LeWitt instruction** gives a round its *rule* (the concept to execute), **Paul
Klee's notebook** gives the *method* — how to make a line actually live. Read both each
round: pick the LeWitt rule, then reach for these Klee principles when deciding HOW the 16
executions should move, balance, and breathe.

> Distilled from Paul Klee, *Beiträge zur bildnerischen Formlehre* (Contributions to a
> Theory of Pictorial Form), Bauhaus lectures 1921–22 — full transcript at
> `../klee/project_resources/paul_klee_text.txt` (German). German terms kept so they can be
> traced back. The image corpus in that folder is Klee's own work, for reference.

## I. The living line
1. **Form is a path, not a result** (*Genesis*). Study the *way* a work came to be, not the
   frozen object — "don't sneak up on a finished result to pluck the front off and run." A
   design is a recorded journey; let the trajectory show.
2. **A point set in motion becomes a line.** Everything pictorial begins at "the point that
   sets itself in movement." Think of every stroke as motion with a temperament.
3. **Take the line for a walk** (*linear aktiv*). A **free** line wanders "for its own sake,
   without a goal" (a stroll); a **purposeful** line hurries point→point→point (an errand —
   it shows as straights). Decide each line's temperament; contrast meander against taut run.
4. **Active / medial / passive** line. *Active* = the line as deed. *Medial* = a line that
   closes a figure (circle, triangle, square) → it stops being movement and becomes a restful
   **plane** ("no one wants to ride a carousel on the rim of the full moon"). *Passive* = a
   line that is merely the **edge left by a plane-action** — suffered, not done. Mix registers.
5. **Line ↔ plane ↔ body.** Move a point → line; move a line → plane; (ideally) move a plane
   → body. An active line silently divides space into *imaginary planes*. On a mono plotter,
   **line density is your tone/plane** — pack lines to imply a filled plane, open them to imply air.

## II. Space, weight, balance
6. **Companion lines; convergence vs. divergence.** A main line with secondary companions —
   *converging* (friends who stay together) or *diverging* (a passer-by who crosses our path
   just once). Perspective is divergence taken to the limit (rail tracks).
7. **The horizon is eye-height** — and beware *Gegenbewegung* (counter-movement). A strong
   vertical/horizontal at eye level anchors the image; stepping off the line swings everything.
8. **Equilibrium, not symmetry** (*die Wage* — the scale). The cross of vertical + horizontal
   is "the spirit of the scale." Balancing **unequal** weights (a big light mass against a
   small heavy one) gives an asymmetric equilibrium that is far livelier than symmetry. Give
   it a **local imperative** (*örtlicher Imperativ*) — a reason the balance must sit *there* —
   so it feels necessary, not arbitrary.
9. **Weight is relative** (*Gewicht*). Heavy/light depends on neighbours and ground: black is
   heavy on white, white is heavy on black, the two only rival on gray. Tiny-and-dense reads
   heavy; large-and-sparse reads light. Weight a composition by playing scale against density.
10. **Centric vs. spinal**, standing vs. lying. One centre = *centric*; stacked centres grow a
    *backbone* (*Rückgrat*). The **vertical** is drama/tension (a tower fighting to not tip);
    the **horizontal** is epic calm (a thing lying down). Choose the posture on purpose.

## III. Rhythm
11. **Structural vs. individual** (*dividuell* vs. *individuell*). *Structural* rhythm =
    repetition of equal units (a grid, wallpaper, bacteria) — divisible, neutral; it enlivens a
    surface without competing. *Individual* rhythm = an organic, **indivisible** whole: remove
    a part and its character dies. Most strong pieces are an individual gesture supported by a
    structural ground.
12. **Prime numbers are the signature of the individual.** Anything that reduces to a repeating
    unit is structural; **irreducible ratios** (2:3:5, 7:11:13:17) refuse to repeat and read as
    organic/alive. To break the deadness of a regular field, space things by primes/irregulars.
13. **Fixed vs. loose articulation** (*feste* vs. *gelöste Gliederung*). Earth-bound, stepwise
    motion is fixed (the staircase, the falling stone); water/air/cosmic motion is loose (the
    swimmer, the free balloon, the meteor). Klee's horizon-of-horizons: *"das Ganze bewegt
    sich"* — the whole moves. Pair a fixed scaffold with a loose, flowing gesture.

## IV. Encounter & growth
14. **The struggle of heterogena: straight vs. circle.** The aggressive **straight** (active,
    "the loosed arrow", *Pfeil*) meets the self-resting **circle**. Their relationship can be
    *struggle* (one yields or breaks), *adaptation* (the straight bends, or the circle relaxes
    into an ellipse), or *mutual* accommodation (both change). Never mere juxtaposition — make
    it an organic encounter, or "the monks and the nuns stay in their two cloisters and nothing
    lives."
15. **Growth and decrease** (*Wachsen und Abnehmen*). A series that swells then shrinks — a
    breathing progression of sizes — turns repetition into life.
16. **Serving vs. ruling** (the organism: bone–tendon–muscle). Build "from serving to ruling":
    give the dominant element room, the connective/structural element little. Subordinate the
    scaffold to the gesture — or deliberately invert it.

## V. Method
17. **Theory comes after.** Klee: theory "only orders things already present to feeling, and in
    the creative process plays only the later role of critique." **Generate by feel first**,
    then use these principles (and the LeWitt rule) to *critique and refine* — never to compute
    a design by formula. The fish of Columbus: the trick is obvious only once someone makes it stand.

## Using this in a round
For each of the 16 executions of the round's LeWitt rule, consciously choose a Klee stance —
e.g. *individual gesture on a structural ground* (11), *asymmetric weight with a local
imperative* (8), *straight-vs-circle adaptation* (14), *fixed scaffold + loose gesture* (13),
*growth series* (15). Note the stance in the design's `intent`. When scoring teaches what
wins, fold it into `LEARNINGS.md` in Klee's vocabulary so the lessons compound.
